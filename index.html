Mapping Crime in Boston: A Spatial and Temporal Analysis

How Do Crime Hotspots, Offense Types, and Time Patterns Shape Violent Crime in Boston?

Kaiyang Weng · Ayaan Imtiaz · Nevan Sehgal

Introduction

Crime is one of the most pressing concerns in urban communities, and understanding where and when it occurs is essential for effective prevention and resource allocation. Boston, as one of the oldest and most densely populated cities in the United States, presents a compelling case study for examining patterns in criminal activity.

With publicly available incident report data from the Boston Police Department, it is now possible to analyze tens of thousands of records to uncover trends that might otherwise go unnoticed. This project explores the spatial and temporal dynamics of crime across Boston's neighborhoods by examining factors such as the time of day incidents occur, which districts experience the highest concentrations of offenses, and what types of crimes are most prevalent in specific areas.

By visualizing these patterns through static and interactive charts, we hope to provide insights that could inform community awareness and contribute to a safer city. Understanding crime at a location-based level is not only academically valuable, but also practically important for residents, policymakers, and law enforcement agencies seeking data-driven approaches to public safety.

A note on data bias: This dataset only represents identified and reported crimes. Many crimes go unreported, and there are well-documented disparities in reporting rates across neighborhoods, demographics, and crime types. The patterns shown here reflect police incident reports, not the full picture of crime in Boston.

About the Data

This dataset is sourced from the Boston Open Data Hub, maintained by the Boston Police Department. It records police incident reports from August 2015 to the present, documenting the where and when of various types of offenses across the city. The original dataset contains approximately 436,666 observations and 17 columns. We use a 10,000-row subset while maintaining all 17 original columns, including DISTRICT, OCCURRED_ON_DATE, YEAR, MONTH, Lat, Long, and Location.

Visualizations

1. Boston Crime Frequency by Day of Week and Hour (Altair)

This heatmap shows crime frequency across days and hours. Hover over any cell to see the exact crime count. Midnight (hour 0) is removed for a cleaner visualization per instructor feedback.

Takeaway: Crime activity peaks during late afternoon and evening hours (3 PM–8 PM) across all days. Fridays and Saturdays show the highest overall activity. Early morning hours (1–5 AM) are consistently quieter. This pattern suggests crime correlates with when people are out and about in the city.

2. Top 5 Crime Types for Top 5 Most Dangerous Streets (Altair)

Grouped bar chart showing the top 5 offense types for the 5 streets with the highest crime counts. Hover over any bar for details.

Takeaway: Washington St dominates with the most incidents by far. Motor vehicle incidents and larceny are common on high-traffic commercial streets, while Blue Hill Ave and Centre St see higher proportions of assault and verbal disputes. The top 5 streets are: Washington St, Blue Hill Ave, Harrison Ave, Gibson St, and Centre St.

3. Top 5 Crimes for Top 5 Districts (Altair)

Grouped bar chart showing the top 5 offense types for the 5 districts with the highest crime counts. Hover for details.

Takeaway: Districts D4 (South End/Back Bay), B2 (Roxbury), A1 (Downtown), C11 (Dorchester), and B3 (Mattapan) are the top 5 crime districts. The types of crime differ: D4 sees more property crimes, while B2 and B3 experience higher rates of assault and violent offenses.

4. Geographic Distribution of Crime Incidents (D3)

Each dot represents a crime incident plotted by latitude and longitude on a map of Boston. District boundaries (derived from incident clusters) are shown as shaded regions. Use the dropdown to filter by district, or toggle to show only shooting incidents. Scroll to zoom; drag to pan. Hover over dots for details.

Takeaway: Crime incidents cluster in downtown Boston (A1/D4), Roxbury (B2), and Dorchester (C11/B3). Shooting incidents, while rare (84 out of 10,000 records), are concentrated along Blue Hill Ave and in the B2/B3 districts. This aligns with Braga, Papachristos, and Hureau's (2010) finding that gun violence in Boston is heavily concentrated at specific micro-locations.

5. Monthly Crime Trends (D3)

Area chart showing crime volume per month. Click and drag on the chart to brush-select a time range and see summary statistics below. Hover over dots to see exact monthly counts.

Takeaway: Crime incidents show a seasonal pattern with higher activity in summer months (June–August) and lower volumes in winter (January–February). This aligns with established criminological research on the relationship between warmer weather and crime rates.

Conclusion

Through this visualization analysis, we explored Boston crime data across spatial, temporal, and categorical dimensions. Our key findings include:

1. Crime is highly concentrated in space and time. A small number of districts (D4, B2, A1, C11) and streets (Washington St, Blue Hill Ave) account for a disproportionate share of incidents. Crime peaks in late afternoon/evening hours and on weekends. This supports Weisburd's (2015) "law of crime concentration" at micro-geographic levels.

2. Crime types vary significantly by location. High-traffic commercial areas see more property crimes and motor vehicle incidents, while residential corridors experience higher rates of violent offenses including shootings.

3. Seasonal patterns are clearly visible. Summer months consistently show higher crime volumes, suggesting opportunities for targeted seasonal public safety interventions.

Future Work

This analysis is limited to a 10,000-row sample primarily from 2023. Future work could expand to the full dataset for multi-year trend analysis, incorporate demographic data (race, gender, income) to examine equity dimensions of crime distribution, overlay university campus boundaries to study campus-area crime patterns, and add population-normalized crime rates per district for fairer comparisons. A time-series analysis accounting for seasonal fluctuations would also be more informative.
